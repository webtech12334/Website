STARS: Structure And Stellar Evolution

A Star is a celestrial body taking the form of Sphere which consists of large amounts of plasma which is held together by its own gravity. Historically, some Stars have been grouped into constellations which allowed astronomers the ability to track the movement of planets. These Stars usually form in groups of dozens to hundreds of thousands, and once formed, they generate extreme heat by continuos nuclear fussion of their hydrogen particles. When Stars exist above a certain size, they continually undergo shell burning where hydrogen is fused into Helium. This eventually leads the Star to becoming red giants and ultimatley helium white dwarfs where they slowly cool after all their hydrogen has been existed.

Stars are originally formed due to gravitational instability within objects with very high density. In fact, once a region approachs a specific density it begins to collapse under its own weight (gravity). This allows stellar ignition where an object first ignites into a flaming ball of gas (i.e. A Star). Other than the extreme heat emitted by all Stars, they also generate what is known as Stellar wind, where streams of particles continually flow from the Star into the surrounding space.

Over billions of years, Stars above a certain size begin to exhaust their hydrogen supply due to the continued nuclear fusion over time. As a result, they begin to fuse hydrogen in a shell outside of their helium core, a phenomenon known as shell burining. This results in the extreme cooling and expanding of their outer shell as they form a Red Giant. Therefore, although they have much larger sizes, Red Giants are cooler than their original structures.

An OverView of Stars:

Approximately 90% of a Stars life is spent fusing hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion at its core. These Stars are said to be in the main sequence of their life-cycle, and are described as dwarf Stars. subsequently, from initial formation, the amount of helium in a Stars core will gradually increase and so will the rate of nuclear fusion, luminosity and temperature of the Star. An example is seen in our own Star, the Sun, which is said to have increased in luminosity by 40% from its formation billions of years ago.

Massive Stars:

A Star with the size of 9 Solar masses (The total mass of our Solar System) or more will for expand into a blue, and then red, giant during its helium-burning phase. In fact, these Stars are likely to evolve into Wolf-Rayet Stars -> Stars with very unusual spectra, extremely heavy elements and emission lines of ionised heliuml, nitrogen and carbon. However, once massive Stars exhaust their helium at the core, they experience a different phenomena to smaller Stars. Specifically, their core contracts, allowing the temperature and pressure to raise high enough to fuse Carbon. After passing through other stages, this continual contraction and fusion results in a Supernova, where the Star explodes, spreading its contents vast distances away from its orginal location.

Binary Stars:

A Binary Star is a system where two Stars orbit around an area, rather than one. Once a Binary Star system reaches its post-main-sequence, its evolutionary path will likely differ significantly from a normal Star of the same mass. This is because if they are close enough, when one becomes a Red Giant it may overflow its Roche Lobe, the area around a Star where all material is gravitationally bound to that Star, allowing material to be transferred to the other Star. This results in many consequences such as the merging of Stars to share their gaseos envelopes if they touch. This is known as contact Binary.

Black holes:

A black hole is a region of space and time which produces such powerful gravitational pull, that nothing, no Stars, Planets, or even electromagentic radiation such as light can escape. In fact, Einsteins theory of general relativity predicts that a mass which is sufficiently compact can deform space-time and produce a black hole. Once formed, this structure has an area known as the event Horizon, where nothing that crosses can escape its gravitational pull. However, although there is almost certainly a physical effect on objects once they cross the event Horizon, no observable effects have been identified.

Structure and Features:

Astronomers have created the 'no-hair conjecture' theory which suggets that once formed, a black hole has three main properties of mass, charge and angular momentum. Other than these, the black hole is a featureless entitiy. As a result, any two black holes that share the same aspect of each feature is indestinguishable from one another. The importance of these three features lies in that they are observable outside of the blackhole. For instance, a charged blackhole will repel other like charges just like any other charged object. This allows astronomers and physicists the ability to study the action and properties of a blackhole.

Event Horizon:

Despite the addionally properties suggested by the 'no-hair conjecture', the defining feature of a blackhole is the event Horizon. This area of spacetime is only present in a blackhole. It is the only point in existence where matter can only cross inwards, and never return outwards. Nothing is capable of escaping the gravitational pull past this point. Not light, planets or any Stars of all sizes. Although this makes a blackhole one of the most interesting objects in existence, it also makes it the most difficult phenomenon study. No information can return to its orginal location once it crosses this point. This makes understanding of what occurs past the event Horizon extremely difficult. Another interesting aspect of the Event Horizon is that once crossed, there are no paths which lead outside the blackhole. This is because according to general relativity, this great mass distorts spacetime so much that the paths take by all particles bends towards the mass. As a result, this becomes so string that no paths lead away from the blackhole. Therefore, theoretically of one was to cross the event Horizon, there would be no path that would allow the individual to simply turn around and travel in the opposite direction.

Singularity:

At the very center of a blackhole, according to relativity, lies a gravitational Singularity. This Singularity is a region where the spacetime curvature becomes infinite. This is also a region of zero volume, but is thought to have an infinite density. It is thought that at the Singularity everything consumed is crushed into this infinite density, adding to the mass of the blackhole. Most researchers believe that this area partially avoids the predictions of relativity, and novel theories of quantum effects must be used to describe the behaviour there.

Dark Matter:

This is a hypothetical form of matter which is thought to account for approx 90% of the matter in the universe, and may consist of a series of undiscovered particles. Although unobservable, dark matter has been inferred from various existing phenomena. For example, there are many gravitational effects which cannot be explained by the laws of gravity without the presence of more mass than is observable in the area. As a result, many researchers believe the existence of dark matter is very likely, and its presence has had a large impact on the evolutionary formation of the universes structure and properties.

Observational Evidence for dark matter:

The main observational eveidence available for dark matter is the rotation of arms around the galactic centre in spirals galaxies. This is primarily because the luminous mass (observable mass) of a spiral galaxy decreases from the outskirts to the centre. Therefore, if the observable mass was all that existed, then we could produce models showing the main point of mass at the centre, with variation around the outskirts, just like in Solar Systems where majority of the mass (the centre mass) is the Star. As a result, one of Keplers many laws show that the rotational velocity should decrease with distance from the centre. This is not the case. In fact, the galaxies rotational speed maintaince consistency as distance increases from the centre. Accordi ng to Keplers laws, the only way to resolve this would be for more (unobservable) mass to exist within the galaxy. Dark Matter.

Galaxy Clusers:

Most researchers have suggested that galaxy clusers are one of the most important phenomena which allows us to study the existence of dark matter. This is because there are three main methods for studying galaxy clusters which generally follow the predictions of dark matter. These techniques consist of studying x-rays emitted from hot gas in the clusters, stuyding the scatter within radial velocities of the galaxies and through gravitational lensing. When evaluating the results of these techniques, researchers have found that they typically agree that dark matter outweighs all visible mass 5 to 1.

Classification:

Dark Matter can be classified into three main cold, warm and hot. However, this refers th velocity rather than actual temperature of the substance. For example, this may measure how far specific objects moved due to random motion in the early universe before they slowed down during cosmic expansion. This distance is termed the free streaming length (FSL). The main difference is that cold dark matter leads to the bottom up formation of structures where the galaxies form first, and galaxy structures later in time. However, hot dark matter results in the top-down formation of structures where large collections of matter form earlier, and later fragment into individual galaxies.

Cosmic Microwave Background:

The cosmic microwave background is electromagentic radiation left over from the formation of the early universe, and is therefore the oldest source of radiation that exists today. Subsequently, although normal matter and dark matter are both types of matter, they do not behave the same. This is primarily evident in the early universe where normal matter was ionised and interacted with radiation strongly. In comparison, dark matter was not ionised but had its main effect on the gravitational potential of objects, as well as the velocity and density of ordinary matter. As a result, their properties evolved differently over time, and therefore had different effects on the cosmic microwave background.
