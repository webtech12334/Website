Uranus

About Uranus:

The seventh planet from the Sun, Uranus is the 4th largest planet in our Solar System when evaulating plantes by mass. Uranus is the most similar to Neptune in terms of chemical composition and physical appearance. As a result, they are classified as 'Ice Giants', compared to Jupiter and Saturn which are 'Gas Giants. Neptune also has the coldest Planetary atmosphere in our Solar System, despite Neptune being further away from the Sun.

  • Name: Derived from the Greek god of the sky Ouranos

Enviromental/Climactic Features:

Similar to Jupiter, Saturn and Neptune, Uranus has no well-defined surface as it is primarily gaseos in nature. Researchers have suggested that at ultraviolet and visible wavelengths, Uranus has the most bland atmosphere of those in our Solar System, and therefore lacks many features otherthan an apparent blurry blue hue. One proposed reason for this lack of detail is that the internal heat of Mercury is much lower than that seen on other giant planets. This was seen when the lowest temperature recorded was -224 degrees celcius, making Uranus the coldest planet in our Solar System.

Formation:

The differences between ice and gas giants remain debatable among researchers. However, many have suggetsted that the difference exists within their formation. For example, the most prominet theory of Planetary formation within our Solar system is from a very large rotating ball gas called a Nebular. It has been suggested that the ice giants, unlike the gas giants, did not absorb enough gas to reach a critical point at which their size would begin increasing exponentially. In addition, researchers examining Planetary migration over the course of our Solar Systems history has suggested that these giant Plants may have formed much closer to the sun than they are today, and migrated furthe out over time.

Planetary Rings:

The rings that surround Uranus consist of very dark particles which vary in size. These sizes range from micrometers, to a fraction of a meter. Currently researchers have identified 13 rings surrounding Uranus, of which the brightest is termed the 'E' ring. Most of these 13 rings are very narrow (approx a few kilometers wide), except two. It is also likely that the ring system is very young, much younger than Uranus itself. In fact, research suggests that the rings did not form with Uranus, but are remnants of a lost moon.

Exploration and Moons:

The voyager 2 probe launched by NASA also encountered Uranus in 1986. This was the first and last flyby to Uranus, and no other missions have been discussed or planned by NASA. Despite this, Voyager 2 was able to study the atmosphere and chemical composition of Uranus. This was accompanied by investigation of all known moons around Uranus, as well as discovering 10 new ones. It also studied the 9 known rings at the time, and discovered two more. All the moons that surround Uranus are named after the works of Shakespear and Alexander pope. Despite this, the mass of the moons around Uranus is the smallest of moons around giant Planets. For example, the mass of 5 of these moons would be less than half of the mass of Triton (the largest moon around Neptune).
