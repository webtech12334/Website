Neptune

About Neptune:

The eigth planet from the Sun, Neptune is the furthest planetary body in our Solar System. Neptune is 17 times larger than Earth, and therefore slightly bigger than its neighbour Uranus. The greater distance of Neptune means that it orbits the Sun once every 164 years. This distance also means Neptune cannot be seen with the naked eye, and is therefore the only planet that was found by mathermatical predictions rather than (telescopic) observations.

General Information

  • Number of moons: 13
  • Date Of Discovery: 1846
  • Rotation Period: 16hrs
  • Planetary Type: Ice Giant
  • Distance From Sun: 4.495 billion km
  • Atmosphere: 80% hydrogen, 19% helium
  • Name: Derived from the Roman god of the sea

Enviromental/Climactic Features:

Although mostly hydrogen and Helium, researchers have found trace amounts of methane in Neptunes atmosphere. This methane results in the blue appearance of Neptune. The methane absorbs any red light that enters the atmosphere, and produces a red hue. However, the weather on the blue planet is another unique feature of Neptune. It is identified by extremely windly storms, where these winds can reach approx 1300mph (almost supersonic speed). These speeds are much greater at the cloud tops and most move in the opposite direction of the planets rotation. The amount of methane present at Neptunes equator is approx 10-100 times that present at its poles.

  • Neptune has a similar storm to venus termed the 'Great Dark Spot'
  • The greater variation in Neptunes weather patterns is partly due to a higher internal temperature
  • On the 11th July 2011, Neptune completed its first orbit since its discovery in 1846
  • Neptunes orbit directly impacts the Kuiper belt (the icy ring at the edge of our solar system)
  • The formation of the two ice giants, Neptune and Uranus, remains debatable although some theories have been suggested

Planetary Rings and Observation:

Rings:

Although not as large as Saturns, and therefore as visible, Neptune also has a planetary ring system. The content of these rings are icy rocks which researchers suggest are coated in carbon-based material, or silicates and results in a partial red hue. These Planetary rings were first detected in 1968, and researchers have since suggested that some of these rings may be incomplete and have gaps.

Observation:

  • Observation has become slightly easy since Neptune brightend significantly between 1980 and 2000.
  • Despite this it is still to dim to be viewed by the naked eye, and other celestrial bodies such as Jupiter's Galilean moons are able to outshine it.
  • If viewed from a Telescope or powerful bonoculos, Neptune will appear as a small blue disk.
  • This far distance means most observatory analysis could not be done until the invention of the hubble Telescope and other very powerful ground based Telescopes.
  • As a result, the first useful ground based observation was done in 1997 from Huwaii

Storms: The Greate Dark Spot

The Great Dark Spot is an atyclonic storm present on Neptune, discovered in 1989 by NASAs Voyager 2 spacecraft. However, when observed 5 years later by the Hubble Telescope, this storm was no longer present, but was replaced by another storm in Neptunes northern Hemisphere. Another storm on Neptune termed 'Scooter' was also discovered in 1989 by Voyager 2, as it was seen moving at faster speeds than estimated for the Great Dark Spot. Additionally, the small dark spot, another cyclonic storm, was discovered by Voyager 2, and although it initially seemed dark, as the spacecraft approached the planet it a bright core developed and the probe recieved images of the phenomenon that can be seen in most high resolution photographs.

Exploration: Voyager 2

Voyager 2 is the only spacecraft to have visited Neptune. As a result of the far distance, signals from the SpaceCraft required 246mins to reach Earth. This meant that most operations performed by the spacecraft were pre-loaded as mission control would be unable to instruct it from such a distance. Therefore, given that Neptune was the last planet that the SpaceCraft could visit, NASA also performed a flyby close to its moons, regardless of what the consequences were to its trajectory. No other missions to Neptune have been conducted since, but the next mission termed the 'Flag ship orbital mission' is thought to take place in the late 2020s or early 2030s.
