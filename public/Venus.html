VENUS

About Venus:

The second planet from the sun, Venus is a similar size to Earth. Venus has the longest Rotation period in our solar system, and rotates in the opposite direction to most other planets. As a result, the sun rises in the west and sets in the East (Opposite to Earth). Venus has no moons and is the second brightest object in the space, after the moon.

General Information

  • Number of moons: 0
  • Date Of Discovery: 1761
  • Rotation Period: 246 days
  • Planetary Type: Terestrial
  • Distance From Sun: 108.2 million km
  • Atmosphere: 96.5% Carbon Dioxide, 3.5% Nitrogen
  • Name: Derived from the Roman goddess of love and Beuty

Enviromental/Climactic Features:

Although sometimes referred to as Earths 'siser' planet due to similar size and distance from the sun, venus has a very dense atmosphere compared to Earths (over 96% CO2). The atmospheric pressure is approximately the same as that found 3000ft underwater on Earth. Venus is by far the hottest planet in the solar system, despite Mercury being closer to the Sun, with an average surface temperature of 462 degrees celcius (735 Kelvin). These extreme temperatures are due to the much thicker atmosphere compared to Mercury and Earth, allowing the temperature to increase by a greenhouse effect. The surface is therefore very dry with no liquid water and highly populated with volcanos.

  • Venus surface is obsurced by thick clouds of sulphuric acid, making visibility from space impossible.
  • The temperature of Venus is higher than that used for sterilisation
  • The surface of Venus is Isothermal: It retains the same temperature on both day and night sides, and between the equator and poles.
  • Venus also tilts on its axis much less than Earth, also contributing to the constant temperature.
  • Although debatable, it is thought the clouds of Venus still may be able to produce lighting.
  • The highest point on Venus (Maxwell Montes) has the coolest temperature of approximately 380 degrees celcius.

Habitability and Life:

The speculation that life existed on Venus decreased significantly in the early 1960s when spacecraft first began studying the harsh atmosphere and realised that conditions were likely far too extreme to support life. For example, a surface temperature of 460 degrees celcius (735K), an atmospheric pressure ninety times that of Earths, and the global impact of the greenhouse effect makes water-based life highly unlikely. However, researchers speculate that it is possible that microorganisms adjusted to the extreme temperatures may live in the cooler and more acidic higher regions of Venus.

Observation: Morning Star and Evening Star

When observed by the naked eye, Venus appears as a white point in the sky that is brighter than any other Star or Planet except the Sun and Moon. Every 584days, Venus overtakes the Earth in its orbit around the Sun. This changes it from the "Evening Star" which is visible after Sunset, to the "Morning Star", which is visible before sunrise.

Facts about Venuses Observation:

  • Venuses bright nature means many have reported it as an "unidentified flying object"
  • When Venus reaches maximum elongation, and becomes its brightest, Venus can be seen in dark skies long after Sunset
  • Venus produces a so-called 'ashen light' (a weak illumination on its dark side), of which was first observed in 1643. This long standing mystery has been speculated to be because of electrical activity in Venuses atmosphere, but could also be illusory due to the observation of a bright, crescent shaped object
  • A cylinder seal found from the Jemdet Nasr period suggests that the ancient Sumerians knew the Morning and Evening star was the same object
  • In comparision, the ancient Egyptians thought the two were different objects and knew them by separate names.
  • The Maya civilisation also knew Venus and considered it the most important of all celestrial bodies, giving it the name 'The Great Star'.

Exploration: Soviet Venra and Mariner 2

The first robotic space probe mission to any planet was performed by the the Soviet Venra in 1961. This lauched towards Venus and was followed by the United States Mariner 2 in 1962. The Mariner 2 marked the first successful interplanatery mission with the probe passing just over 21,000 miles above the surface, collecting data about Venuses atmosphere.

Early missions to Venus

  • Soviet Venra 4 (18th October 1967): Entered the atmosphere of Venus and discovered that the surface temperature was much hotter than Mariner 2 predicted, and the atmosphere much more dense
  • Mariner 10 (1974): Flew pass Venus on the way to Mercury and captured ultraviolet photographs of extreme wind speeds
  • Soviet Venra 9 and 10 (1975): Transmitted the first black and white images of the surface of Venus
  • Soviet Venra 13 and 14 (1982): The first color images of the surface were obtained
